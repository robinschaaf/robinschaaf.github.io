With a UX interest

I've long been a developer, but by far I'm best at figuring out how to use software to solve problems for users. This is what drove me to choose to get into computers in the first place when I was in high school in a retail job - we used a poorly designed point-of-sale system and I knew I could come up with better softrware than that. (As an illustration - the F5 key would complete the order - but right next to it the F4 key would void the sale, and without verifying first. It is incredibly frustrating to accidentally cancel an order and have to re-ring everything, in front of the customer. Seriously, though, who would do this ?!)

In 2010 I decided to get my Master's degree in Human-Computer Interaction from DePaul, after having written and open sourcing a successful electronic resources management system (ERMS), for which I invested a lot into learning about usability and understanding the problemset and workflow, and developed it using an iterative design process. It paid off for the project - the ERMS has been adopted and is in use in about 50 confirmed institutions (many more unknown), and even has a handful of developers from other institutions that continue to contribute and maintain to the codebase on github. As a developer, it has been incredibly gratifying that to find out about user groups and presentations about the software being done at conferences that I'm not even a part of!
The project also cemented my interest in usability and design - because without a doubt, it wasn't the code that drove CORAL's success - but that users enjoy using the interface and it solves the types of issues they face every day. (though, perhaps the flexible data model helps :-) )
Anyway, in February 2014 I changed my job at the library to focus on library UX. I've finished about two-thirds of my master's degree and will probably be slowly chugging away at it for another year or two.
